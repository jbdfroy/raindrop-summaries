    Title: The Challenge to U.S. Dollar Dominance from Digital Alternatives Source: Foreign Policy (July 2025)

    Article:

    The dominance of the US dollar as the world’s primary reserve currency has faced a new challenge with the rise of digital alternatives, particularly stablecoins. Trump, initially skeptical about cryptocurrencies, has now embraced them during his second term, promoting U.S. dollar stablecoins backed by cash and other safe dollar assets to reinforce the dollar’s dominance.

    While unbacked cryptocurrencies can be volatile, stablecoins promise reduced risk, lower transaction costs for multinational firms, and financial services to underbanked populations. If the market expands dramatically and remains dollar-denominated, it could strengthen the dollar’s position and U.S. influence.

    However, this benefit is not guaranteed. Stablecoin issuers can be based anywhere, making enforcement of regulations complex, especially with Trump’s administration’s policy unpredictability and hostile approach to trade. Foreign governments may turn a blind eye to unscrupulous operators or favor their own central bank digital currencies, potentially leading to fragmentation of global financial markets.

    Trump has conflicting views on whether dollar dominance serves U.S. national interest. While he argues that the dollar’s preeminent role hurts U.S. businesses’ competitiveness, markets continue to support it due to the deepest and most sophisticated U.S. financial markets, innovative companies, and independent courts and regulators.

    Despite the challenges, the Genius Act provides a regulatory framework for dollar stablecoins in the U.S., imposing transparency rules, independent audits, and requiring one-to-one backing with cash, Treasuries, or other high-quality liquid assets. However, the growing competition may lead to pressure to bend the rules, potentially weakening financial stability and setting interest rates in various countries.

    The future of stablecoins could lead to more fragmented financial markets, requiring U.S. regulators to build tighter relationships with their counterparts in key foreign jurisdictions to ensure that dollar stablecoins issued there are fully backed and that U.S. rules and sanctions are reliably enforced. If enforcement becomes disjointed, it may undermine global cooperation on sanctions enforcement and weaken the position of the US dollar.
