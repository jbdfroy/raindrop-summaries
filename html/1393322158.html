    The article discusses the strategic maneuvers of various world leaders, particularly those from Europe and Asia, in relation to U.S. President Donald Trump during his second term. The focus is on how these leaders, specifically Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, managed their relationships with Trump to influence U.S. policy towards Asia, particularly in relation to China.

    The article begins by comparing the meeting between Trump and Russian President Vladimir Putin in Alaska with a subsequent meeting involving Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky, along with leaders from Finland, France, Germany, Italy, the United Kingdom, and the European Union. This joint meeting was seen as a strategic move to counterbalance Trump’s apparent pro-Russia stance and to secure U.S. support for Ukraine against Russia’s invasion.

    The article then delves into Abe’s successful efforts to influence Trump during his first term, particularly in shaping the U.S.’s Asia strategy. Abe was able to convince Trump to adopt Japan’s “free and open Indo-Pacific” framework and to soften his stance towards North Korea.

    However, in Trump’s second term, Asian leaders have struggled to manage their alliances with him. The absence of a leader like Abe, who resigned due to illness and was assassinated in 2021, has left a gap that needs to be filled. The article discusses the strategies of current leaders from Australia, South Korea, and India in dealing with Trump, highlighting the importance of personal relationships, flattery, persuasion, and political alignment.

    The article concludes by emphasizing the significance of maintaining U.S. engagement in Asia, given China’s ambitions in the region. It suggests that Asian leaders should learn from their European counterparts and ensure that the U.S. remains an active player in regional affairs.
